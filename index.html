Property Selection & Justification

Tampa · $400k SFH

I select a single-family home in Tampa, Florida with a purchase price of $400,000. The property is in a residential neighborhood with good schools, access to jobs, and strong rental demand.

Why Tampa?
  • One of the faster-growing metropolitan areas in Florida.
  • Strong labor market and steady population inflows.
  • Attractive climate and lifestyle support long-run housing demand.
  • Home prices have historically outperformed the national average.

These fundamentals support both rent growth and long-run appreciation potential.

Why a Single-Family Home?
  • Offers more privacy and space than condos, attracting families and long-term renters.
  • Typically exhibits higher appreciation than smaller units in similar areas.
  • Fewer HOA constraints and surprise assessments than many condominiums.
  • Fits naturally within the project’s $200,000–$500,000 price range.
Affordability & Growth

A $400,000 price point is high enough to participate in Tampa’s appreciation but still attainable with a 20% down payment. Historical data for Tampa show significant price increases over the past decade, suggesting that long-term owners can build equity through both principal paydown and appreciation.

Resale Value & Bond-Style Return

Real Estate vs Stocks & Bonds

I treat the property as a fixed-income style investment by combining the initial cash outflows (down payment and closing costs), the net rental income, and the future resale price into a stream of cash flows, and then compute a bond-equivalent internal rate of return (IRR).

Resale Value Assumption

I assume a conservative long-run home price appreciation rate (for example, around 3–4% per year over 30 years). Under this assumption, the $400,000 Tampa home is worth substantially more at the end of the mortgage term.

Combined with principal paydown, this creates meaningful homeowner equity by year 30.

Bond-Equivalent Return

Interpreting:

  • Down payment + closing costs as the initial “bond price,”
  • Net monthly cash flows as coupon payments, and
  • Net equity from resale as the terminal value,

the implied bond-style IRR is approximately in the high single-digit range (around 9–10% annually), depending on the exact appreciation and rent growth assumptions.

Comparison with Stocks

Long-run stock market returns (e.g., S&P 500) are historically around 8–10% per year but with high volatility and no direct consumption benefit. The real estate investment’s estimated IRR is competitive with equities, though it is less diversified and less liquid.

Comparison with Long-Term Bonds

Over the same horizon, long-term Treasuries and investment-grade bonds usually offer lower expected returns than equities. The property’s projected IRR appears higher than long-term bond yields, but with additional risks (local housing cycles, maintenance shocks, vacancy) and the leverage embedded in the mortgage.

Overall, the Tampa house behaves like a leveraged bond with embedded options (prepayment, default, and sale timing). Its expected return can exceed that of long-term bonds while remaining competitive with equity returns, provided the rental market stays healthy and the property is well managed.

Question 4

Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac & Mortgage-Backed Securities

GSEs & MBS
Who Are Fannie Mae & Freddie Mac?

Fannie Mae (1938) and Freddie Mac (1970) are government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) whose mission is to provide liquidity, stability, and affordability to the U.S. housing market.

They do not typically originate mortgages themselves. Instead, they buy conforming loans from lenders, guarantee them, and bundle them into mortgage-backed securities (MBS).

How MBS Are Created
  • Banks originate conforming residential mortgages.
  • Fannie or Freddie purchases eligible loans.
  • Loans are pooled into a diversified portfolio.
  • The pool is securitized into MBS and sold to investors.
  • Investors receive interest and principal from the underlying mortgages.
Risks for MBS Investors
  • Prepayment risk – borrowers refinance or prepay when rates fall.
  • Extension risk – prepayments slow when rates rise, extending effective duration.
  • Interest-rate risk – changes in rates move MBS values up or down.
  • Credit risk – some borrowers may default on their mortgages.
  • Liquidity risk – complex or lower-rated MBS can be harder to trade quickly.
Role in the 2007–2009 Crisis

Before the financial crisis, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guaranteed and invested in large portfolios of mortgages and MBS linked to a rapidly inflating housing market. When home prices reversed and defaults increased, they experienced heavy losses.

In 2008, both GSEs were placed into federal conservatorship. Since then, underwriting standards have tightened, capital rules have been strengthened, and their activities are more closely supervised.

Today, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac still guarantee a large share of U.S. mortgages. Their guarantees help keep mortgage rates lower and the market more liquid, while also concentrating housing-finance risk in two government-linked institutions.

Question 5

Treasury Rates, Spreads & Housing

1Y, 10Y & 30Y (2005–2025)

Using FRED data from January 2005 to June 2025 for the 1-year, 10-year, and 30-year Treasury constant-maturity rates, I identify several phases in the interest-rate environment.

2005–2007
Short-term rates rose toward 5% as the Fed tightened policy before the Global Financial Crisis. The yield curve flattened and inverted, signaling concerns about a slowdown.
2008–2015
Post-crisis, policy rates fell near zero and remained extremely low. Long-term yields also dropped, supported by quantitative easing and safe-haven demand.
2016–2019
A normalization phase: the Fed gradually raised rates, and the curve again flattened as short-term yields moved closer to long-term yields.
2020
The pandemic shock pushed yields to record lows. The 10-year briefly traded near 0.5%, while the 1-year hovered near zero under emergency easing.
2022–2023
High inflation prompted aggressive Fed hikes. Yields rose sharply across the curve, and inversions of intermediate spreads reached historic depths.
Mid-2025
Yields partially normalized: the 1-year around 4%, the 10-year around 4.2%, and the 30-year near 4.8%, leaving a flatter but still upward-sloping curve.

Implications for Mortgage Rates & Housing Prices

1
Q4 2025 Mortgage Rate Outlook
With flatter spreads, moderating inflation, and signs of slower growth, it is more likely that mortgage rates stabilize or drift slightly lower rather than rise sharply again in Q4 2025.
2
Impact of Higher Rates on Housing
If mortgage rates did move significantly higher, affordability would worsen, buyer demand would weaken, and sellers might need to cut prices or offer concessions. Rate-sensitive segments, like first-time buyers, would be hit hardest.

Because mortgage rates are closely benchmarked to long-term Treasury yields plus a spread, the shape and level of the yield curve directly influence housing affordability, construction, and home prices.

Mortgage Spreads & Regression on Treasury Spreads

ARM vs Fixed · Yield Curve

For Question 6, I use FRED data from January 2005 to June 2025 on:

  • 5/1-year adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) rates,
  • 15-year fixed mortgage rates, and
  • 30-year fixed mortgage rates.

From these series, I construct two mortgage spreads:

  • Spread 1: 15-year fixed − 5/1 ARM
  • Spread 2: 30-year fixed − 15-year fixed

I then regress each spread on two Treasury yield-curve spreads:

  • ts_10_1: 10-year − 1-year Treasury yield
  • ts_30_1: 30-year − 1-year Treasury yield
Regression 1: (15Y − 5/1 ARM)

The first regression relates the spread between the 15-year fixed mortgage rate and the 5/1 ARM rate to the two Treasury spreads. The coefficients on the Treasury spreads are statistically meaningful but modest, and the R-squared is relatively low.

Interpretation: only a small share of this mortgage spread is explained by the slope of the yield curve alone. Bank funding costs, prepayment behavior, and product-specific risk premiums also play large roles.

Regression 2: (30Y − 15Y)

The second regression models the spread between the 30-year and 15-year fixed mortgage rates using the same Treasury spreads. Here, the coefficients are larger in magnitude and the R-squared is higher than in Regression 1.

Interpretation: the extra premium for extending from a 15-year to a 30-year fixed mortgage is more tightly linked to the long-end slope of the Treasury curve, reflecting compensation for additional duration and prepayment uncertainty.

Overall, the regressions confirm that Treasury spreads help explain mortgage spreads, but they do not fully determine them. Mortgage markets embed additional layers of risk, optionality, and institutional behavior on top of the Treasury curve.
